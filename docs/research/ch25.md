**Thesis:** Race, coloniality, and gender are among the most consequential doxonomic systems. The framework captures their institutional reproduction; its limitations (non-monetary reproduction, embodied belief, positionality) are acknowledged explicitly.
**Companion:** `docs/research/book.md`, especially §3 on non-English traditions.

Ch. 25 is the chapter where the book's Anglo-Saxon default is most at risk of becoming a hole in the argument. It already cites Fanon, Said, Spivak, Quijano, Mignolo, Fricker, Medina, Butler, Connell, Crenshaw, Jost, and Du Bois. The live question is whether the dossier should be expanded with more non-English and non-Anglophone sources, and whether the decolonial / Latin American tradition in particular should be strengthened.

## 6. Open questions specific to Ch. 25

1. **The non-monetary doxonomic accounting framework.** The chapter acknowledges that gender and racial regimes are reproduced through unpaid labor and institutional routine; this is the framework's most honest limitation. A parallel formalism for "temporal investment" or "institutional-routine reproduction" is open research. Ch. 14 (belief-flow accounting) would be the natural home.
2. **Cross-regional empirical calibration.** The racial belief production model (L58–70) is parameterized for US-like contexts. Applying it to South African apartheid → post-apartheid, Indian caste, Brazilian *mestizaje* → colorism, and French *laïcité* → racialized Muslim Otherness would test the framework's generality. Each is a paper-length project.
3. **The positionality footnote.** §"Limitations" (L288–321) acknowledges the positionality problem. A specific reflection on the book's own position would be worth a short methodological addendum; the current discussion is abstract.
4. **The sheaf-theoretic intersectionality example (§26.2.6)** is mathematically suggestive but not fully worked out. Whether $H^1 \neq 0$ can be computed for a concrete set of local belief systems is an open demonstration the chapter alludes to but does not perform.
5. **Non-English reception of the book.** If the book is translated into Spanish, Portuguese, French, Arabic, or Chinese, the Ch. 25 citations will need to engage more deeply with scholarship in those languages. Planning for that translation round is out of scope here but should shape the P1 non-English additions.
