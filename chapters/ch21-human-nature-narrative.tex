\chapter{Human Nature as a Managed Narrative}
\label{ch:human-nature}

\epigraph{It is not that people are either good or bad by nature, but that they can be made more selfish or more cooperative by the stories they are told about themselves.}{Samuel Bowles}

\noindent
This is the signature chapter of the book.
The belief that ``human nature is selfish'' is among the most consequential doxonomic products of the modern era: an anthropological regime reinforced through centuries of institutional investment, whose self-fulfilling properties make it appear to confirm itself.
Whether it is \emph{the} most consequential---more than racial hierarchy, national myth, or religious cosmology---is a comparative question this chapter does not attempt to settle.
What it does attempt is a detailed doxonomic analysis of one regime whose mechanisms are well-documented and whose effects are measurable.

\section{The Claim and the Evidence}
\label{sec:ch21-evidence}

The dominant anthropological regime in Western market societies holds that:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item humans are fundamentally self-interested,
  \item cooperation requires external enforcement (contracts, laws, incentives),
  \item altruism is either disguised self-interest or evolutionarily marginal.
\end{enumerate}

The experimental evidence does not support these claims as a complete anthropology, though it does not deny that self-interest, status competition, and opportunism are real human tendencies.
\textcite{fischbacher2001} showed that the majority of participants in public goods games are \emph{conditional cooperators}: they cooperate when they expect others to cooperate.
Only about 30\% behave as consistent free riders.
\textcite{henrich2001} demonstrated cross-cultural generosity in ultimatum and dictator games---people in diverse societies routinely reject pure self-interest, though the degree varies with institutional context.
\textcite{bowles2011} synthesized evidence that cooperation is a fundamental, evolved human capacity, coexisting with competition and conflict rather than replacing them.
\textcite{ostrom1990} documented communities successfully managing commons without external enforcement for centuries.

The doxonomic point is not that humans are ``fundamentally cooperative'' (which would be an equal and opposite oversimplification) but that the dominant regime selectively amplifies one real tendency while suppressing evidence of others.
The best available anthropology is that humans are \emph{conditionally cooperative}: capable of both selfishness and solidarity, with the balance depending heavily on institutional design, narrative environment, and perceived norms \autocite{gintis2005}.

\section{The Doxonomic Construction of Homo Economicus}
\label{sec:ch21-homo-economicus}

\begin{definition}[Homo Economicus as Anthropological Regime]
\label{def:homo-economicus}
\index{homo economicus}
\emph{Homo economicus} is the anthropological regime $\alpha_{\text{HE}}$ defined by:
motivational theory = rational self-interest,
social ontology = isolated individuals,
capacity claim = collective action requires external incentives.
\end{definition}

This regime did not become dominant through evidence.
It became dominant through institutional investment:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item economics curricula teach self-interest as the behavioral foundation (over 10 million students per decade in the U.S.\ alone),
  \item management theory adopts principal-agent models premised on worker selfishness,
  \item policy design assumes self-interest (incentive-based regulation, privatization),
  \item popular culture reinforces competition narratives (``survival of the fittest'').
\end{itemize}

\section{The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy}
\label{sec:ch21-self-fulfilling}

\begin{theorem}[Doxonomic Self-Fulfillment]
\label{thm:self-fulfilling}
\index{self-fulfilling prophecy}
Let agents be conditional cooperators who cooperate when $\hat{\mu}_j(\text{cooperate}) > \tau_j$.
If a doxonomic intervention reduces $\hat{\mu}_j(\text{cooperate})$ by shifting perceived norms, then:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item fewer agents cooperate,
  \item this confirms the narrative (``see, people are selfish''),
  \item which further reduces $\hat{\mu}_j(\text{cooperate})$,
  \item producing a downward spiral until the low-cooperation equilibrium is reached.
\end{enumerate}
Formally, the dynamics satisfy $\mu^{(t+1)}_{\text{coop}} = G(\hat{\mu}^{(t)}_{\text{coop}})$ where $G$ is increasing and $\hat{\mu}^{(t)}$ is biased downward by the doxonomic intervention.
\end{theorem}

This is the most dangerous property of selfishness narratives: they generate the evidence for their own truth.

\section{Two Competing Regimes}
\label{sec:ch21-competition}

\begin{model}[Anthropological Regime Competition]
\label{mod:regime-competition}
\index{anthropological regime!competition}
Let $\belief{S}(t)$ be the prevalence of the selfish regime and $\belief{C}(t) = 1 - \belief{S}(t)$ the cooperative alternative.
The dynamics are:
\begin{align}
  \frac{d\belief{S}}{dt} &= \belief{S}\bigl(\alpha_S \phi_S - \gamma_S - \beta_{SC}\belief{C}\bigr) \\
  \frac{d\belief{C}}{dt} &= \belief{C}\bigl(\alpha_C \phi_C - \gamma_C - \beta_{CS}\belief{S}\bigr)
\end{align}
where $\phi_S \gg \phi_C$ reflects the massive funding asymmetry.
Even if the cooperative regime has lower intrinsic decay ($\gamma_C < \gamma_S$) and stronger peer reinforcement ($\beta_{CS} < \beta_{SC}$), the funding advantage can ensure selfish-regime dominance.
\end{model}

\begin{proposition}[Funding Threshold for Regime Change]
\label{prop:funding-threshold}
The cooperative regime displaces the selfish regime if and only if
\[
  \alpha_C \phi_C > \gamma_C + \beta_{CS} \cdot \belief{S}^*
\]
where $\belief{S}^*$ is the current selfish-regime prevalence.
This gives a concrete estimate of the \emph{counter-doxonomic investment} required for regime change.
\end{proposition}

\section{Exercises}

\begin{exercise}
Using Proposition~\ref{prop:funding-threshold}, estimate the annual counter-doxonomic investment needed to shift the U.S.\ anthropological regime from selfish to cooperative.
State your assumptions explicitly.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design an experiment to test the self-fulfilling prophecy of selfishness narratives.
Randomly expose groups to different anthropological narratives and measure cooperation in subsequent public goods games.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Construct a doxonomic account (Chapter~\ref{ch:belief-flow-accounting}) for the belief ``human nature is selfish,'' identifying at least five funding channels and estimating their annual expenditure.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Argue that the self-fulfilling property of selfishness beliefs creates a \emph{doxonomic trap}: a locally stable equilibrium that is globally suboptimal.
Under what conditions can the trap be escaped?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Compare the doxonomic investment in ``humans are selfish'' with the investment in ``humans are cooperative.''
What is the funding ratio?
What does this imply about the epistemic status of the dominant regime?
\end{exercise}
